REITs Explained: Real Estate for Rs 300

A Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT) is a company that owns, operates, or finances income-generating real estate. In India, REITs let retail investors own premium commercial properties - the same buildings where TCS, Infosys, and Goldman Sachs have their offices - for the price of a pizza.

Think of REITs Like This

Imagine 10,000 investors pooling money to buy a Rs 1,000 crore office tower in Bangalore. The rent from companies like Microsoft and Accenture is collected and distributed to all investors. That's a REIT - but you can own your share for just Rs 300.

SEBI REIT Requirements

  • 80% rule: At least 80% must be invested in completed, income-generating properties
  • 90% distribution: Must distribute 90% of net distributable cash flow to unitholders
  • Debt limit: Cannot exceed 49% of asset value
  • January 2026 change: REITs reclassified as equity instruments (mutual funds can now invest)

Why REITs Beat Direct Real Estate

Liquidity High (sell in seconds) Low (months to years)

Tenant Issues Not your problem Your daily headache
Key Insight: Residential rental yields in India hover between 2-3% in most metros. REITs offer 6-8% yields from commercial properties that individual investors typically cannot access. Plus, no 2 AM calls about leaking pipes!

Tax Implications 2026: Gold & REITs

Tax rules for gold and REITs have changed significantly, especially after Budget 2025. Here's what you need to know.

Gold Type Holding Period Tax Rate

Important Change (Budget 2025): No indexation benefit for any gold investment. Flat 12.5% LTCG rate applies. This makes SGBs' tax-free maturity even more valuable.

REIT Taxation (2026)

Component Tax Treatment
Dividend Income Slab rate (if SPV uses 115BAA regime); Exempt if SPV pays normal tax
Interest Income Taxed at slab rate

Key Insight: REITs issue Form 64B annually breaking down distribution components (interest, dividend, rent, capital repayment) for accurate tax filing. Keep this document for ITR.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake #1: Buying Gold Jewelry for Investment

Why it's a problem: Making charges (15%+), GST (3%), wastage (2%), and buy-back discounts (5%) mean you lose Rs 15,000-25,000 per Rs 1 lakh invested before gold even moves.

Better approach: Buy jewelry for ceremonies (as an expense), invest in SGBs or Gold ETFs for wealth creation.

Mistake #2: Ignoring SGB Tax Benefits

Why it's a problem: Gold ETF gains are taxed at 12.5% LTCG, while SGB maturity gains are completely tax-free. On Rs 10 lakh gain, that's Rs 1.25 lakh saved!

Better approach: Prioritize SGBs for long-term gold allocation (5+ years), use Gold ETFs for tactical/short-term needs.

Mistake #3: Over-Allocating to Gold After Rally

Why it's a problem: Gold surged 74% in 2025. Chasing past performance often leads to buying at peaks. Gold is volatile and can stagnate for years (2013-2018).

Better approach: Stick to 5-10% allocation regardless of recent performance. Rebalance if gold exceeds target.

Mistake #4: Treating REITs as "Safe" Like FDs

Why it's a problem: REITs trade on stock exchanges and can drop 15-20% during market corrections. They're not fixed income instruments.

Better approach: Treat REITs as hybrid instruments - income characteristics of bonds but volatility closer to stocks. Don't invest emergency funds.

Mistake #5: Ignoring REIT Distribution Tax Complexity

Why it's a problem: REIT distributions have multiple components (dividend, interest, capital return) with different tax treatments. Wrong filing can trigger notices.

Better approach: Use Form 64B issued by REIT for accurate component-wise reporting in your ITR.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best way to invest in gold in India?

Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs) are the most tax-efficient option, offering 2.5% annual interest plus gold price appreciation with zero capital gains tax at 8-year maturity. For liquidity, Gold ETFs are recommended with 0.40% expense ratios and 12-month holding for LTCG benefits. SGBs can be bought from secondary markets on NSE/BSE since new issues have stopped.

How can I invest in REITs in India with small amounts?

You can buy REIT units on stock exchanges (NSE/BSE) through your demat account for as low as Rs 300-500 per unit. The minimum lot size is just 1 unit since SEBI's 2021 amendment. Embassy, Mindspace, Brookfield, and Nexus Select are the four main REITs listed in India, offering 5-8% dividend yields.

What returns do Indian REITs provide?

Indian REITs offer dividend yields of 5-8% annually from rental income, plus potential capital appreciation. Embassy REIT yields 5.49%, while Mindspace has delivered 52% total returns over 5 years. REITs must distribute 90% of net distributable cash flow to unitholders by SEBI regulation.

Is gold a good hedge against stock market crashes?

Historically yes - gold gained 69% when NIFTY crashed 35% in February 2009 during the subprime crisis. During COVID crash in March 2020, gold gained 25% while Sensex fell 13.2%. Gold's 5-year CAGR of 17.2% also beat NIFTY 50's 16.89% during 2019-2024. Financial experts recommend 5-10% gold allocation as portfolio insurance.

What are the tax implications of gold and REITs in 2026?

Gold ETFs: 12.5% LTCG after 12 months holding. Physical gold: 12.5% LTCG after 24 months. SGBs: Completely tax-free at 8-year maturity. REITs: 12.5% LTCG after 12 months (above Rs 1.25 lakh exemption), 20% STCG for shorter holdings, dividends taxed at your slab rate if SPV uses concessional tax regime.

Why have new Sovereign Gold Bond issues stopped?

No new SGB tranches have been issued for FY 2024-25 or FY 2025-26 as the government aims to reduce debt. However, existing SGBs continue until maturity with all benefits intact. You can still buy SGBs through the secondary market on NSE/BSE, typically at slight premium or discount to the issue price.

What is the difference between Gold ETF and Sovereign Gold Bonds?

Gold ETFs offer high liquidity with same-day selling and 12-month LTCG at 12.5% tax, but no interest income. SGBs offer 2.5% annual interest plus complete tax exemption at 8-year maturity, but have a 5-year minimum lock-in (with exit windows). SGBs are better for long-term investors; Gold ETFs for those needing liquidity.
