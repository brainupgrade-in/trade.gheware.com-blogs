What Are Debt Instruments?

Debt instruments are loans you give to governments or companies, and they pay you interest in return. Unlike equity where you own a piece of the company, with debt you're a lender - entitled to fixed interest payments and return of principal.

Think of Debt Like Being a Bank

When you buy a government bond, you're lending money to the Government of India. When you buy a corporate bond, you're lending to that company. They pay you interest - just like a bank pays you interest on your FD.

Government Securities (G-Secs) Explained

Government Securities are debt instruments issued by the Government of India to finance its fiscal deficit. They carry sovereign guarantee - the highest level of safety for any investment in India. If G-Secs default, it means India as a country has defaulted.

Corporate Bonds & Credit Risk

Corporate bonds are debt instruments issued by companies to raise capital. They typically offer higher yields than government bonds but come with credit risk - the possibility that the company may not pay back.

Key Risks in Corporate Bonds

1. Credit Default Risk

The issuer may be unable to pay interest or return principal. Even AA-rated companies can default. Always check if the company can sustain payouts from operating cash flow.

2. Interest Rate Risk

Bond prices fall when interest rates rise. A 10-year bond can lose 8-10% value if rates rise by 1%. This matters if you need to sell before maturity.

3. Liquidity Risk

Corporate bonds can be hard to sell, especially during market stress. You may have to accept a lower price or wait long periods to find a buyer.

India's corporate bond market has grown from Rs 16 trillion in 2014 to Rs 51.58 trillion in 2024. Yet corporate bond awareness among retail investors is just 6-13%, and penetration is less than 1%. The opportunity is massive for informed investors.

Franklin Templeton Crisis: Lessons Learned

In April 2020, Franklin Templeton wound up 6 debt schemes, freezing Rs 30,000 crore of investor money. This is the most important case study in Indian debt fund history.

What Happened?

The 6 schemes wound up were: Low Duration, Dynamic Accrual, Credit Risk, Short Term Income, Ultra Short, and Income Opportunities.

Root Causes:

  1. Illiquid Credit Instruments: The funds held lower-rated corporate bonds that became impossible to sell during COVID panic
  2. Redemption Pressure: Scared investors rushed to withdraw money simultaneously
  3. Liquidity Mismatch: Daily redeemable funds holding securities that take weeks/months to sell
The Devastating Impact: "Winding up means that investors who had put money into those six Franklin Templeton schemes could no longer simply withdraw their cash. Instead, the money was frozen. Investors of some schemes needed to wait five years to recover their money fully." - Business Today

Lessons for Investors

Lesson 1: Credit Quality Matters

Even "safe" debt funds carry credit risk. Stick to funds investing in AAA-rated securities or government bonds for true safety.

Lesson 2: Avoid Credit Risk Funds for Core Debt

Credit risk funds are NOT safe haven investments. Use them only for a small satellite allocation if at all.

Lesson 3: Check Portfolio Holdings

Before investing, check what a debt fund actually holds. Avoid funds with significant exposure to below-AA rated securities.

Post-Franklin crisis, liquid funds and overnight funds maintained stability. G-Secs and AAA-rated bonds recovered quickly. The lesson: sovereign guarantee or nothing for your core debt allocation.

Tax Changes After 2023 (Critical)

Post-April 2023, debt funds lost their tax advantage completely. This is perhaps the most important change affecting debt fund investors in a decade.

The Big Change: April 1, 2023

Before April 2023

  • Holding > 36 months = LTCG
  • Tax rate: 20% with indexation
  • Indexation adjusted for inflation
  • Made debt funds superior to FDs for high earners

After April 2023

  • ALL gains at slab rate
  • No LTCG benefit regardless of holding
  • No indexation benefit
  • Same taxation as FDs now

Impact: Real Math for 30% Tax Bracket

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake #1: Treating All Debt as "Safe"

Why it's a problem: Credit risk funds, AA-rated corporate bonds, and lower-grade debt can lose money - as Franklin Templeton investors learned

Better approach: Use only G-Secs, AAA-rated bonds, or gilt funds for core debt allocation

Mistake #2: Ignoring Interest Rate Risk

Why it's a problem: Long-duration bonds/funds can lose 8-10% if interest rates rise 1%. That's equity-like volatility.

Better approach: Match duration to your investment horizon. Short goals = short duration funds.

Mistake #3: Chasing Higher Yields

Why it's a problem: A 10% yielding bond seems better than 7%, but the extra 3% comes with higher default risk

Better approach: Remember: higher yield = higher risk. If it seems too good, investigate why.

Mistake #4: Using Debt Funds Post-2023 Without Tax Analysis

Why it's a problem: Debt funds no longer have tax advantage over FDs for most investors

Better approach: For high tax brackets, consider direct G-Secs via RBI Retail Direct (no TDS)

Mistake #5: Zero Debt Allocation When Young

Why it's a problem: Even young investors need stability during crashes to avoid panic selling equity at the bottom

Better approach: Maintain at least 20-25% in debt even at age 25. It's your emotional anchor during volatility.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the safest debt instruments for Indian retail investors?

Government Securities (G-Secs) and RBI Floating Rate Savings Bonds offer sovereign guarantee with zero credit risk. G-Secs currently yield 6.6-6.7% for 10-year bonds, while RBI bonds offer 8.05% for Jan-Jun 2026. These are backed by the Government of India - the safest debt instruments available.

How do I buy government bonds directly in India?

Open a free Retail Direct Gilt (RDG) account at rbiretaildirect.org.in using PAN and Aadhaar. Minimum investment is Rs 10,000 for G-Secs, T-Bills, and SDLs. You can participate in primary auctions and secondary market trading with zero account maintenance fees.

Are debt mutual funds still tax-efficient after 2023?

No. Post-April 2023, all debt fund gains are taxed at your income slab rate regardless of holding period. Indexation benefits have been removed. For high tax bracket investors, debt funds now offer similar tax treatment to FDs, making direct G-Sec investment potentially more attractive.

What is the difference between liquid funds and gilt funds?

Liquid funds invest in securities with up to 91-day maturity (minimal interest rate risk, 5.8-7% returns), ideal for emergency funds with next-day redemption. Gilt funds invest in government securities of various tenures (higher interest rate risk, 6.6-8% returns), suitable for 3+ year horizons when interest rates are expected to fall.

How much of my portfolio should be in debt?

Use the Rule of 100: subtract your age from 100 for equity allocation, rest in debt. A 30-year-old might have 70% equity, 30% debt. Research shows a 60:40 equity-debt portfolio in India delivered 13.5% CAGR over 20 years with significantly lower volatility than 100% equity portfolios.

What happened in the Franklin Templeton debt fund crisis?

In April 2020, Franklin Templeton wound up 6 debt schemes, freezing Rs 30,000 crore of investor money affecting 3 lakh investors. The crisis was caused by illiquid credit instruments combined with redemption pressure during COVID panic. It took 4-5 years for investors to recover their money fully. The lesson: stick to AAA-rated or government securities for true safety.

What is the current RBI Floating Rate Bond interest rate?

RBI Floating Rate Savings Bonds 2020 (Taxable) offer 8.05% interest for January-June 2026. The rate is calculated as NSC rate (7.70%) plus 35 basis points spread, and is reset every 6 months. The bonds have a 7-year lock-in period with premature exit options available for senior citizens.
