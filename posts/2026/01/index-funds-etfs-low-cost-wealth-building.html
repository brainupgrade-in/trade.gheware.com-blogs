What Are Index Funds?

An index fund is a mutual fund that tracks a market index like Nifty 50 by investing in the same stocks in the same proportion. Instead of paying a fund manager to pick stocks, you simply "buy the market" at the lowest possible cost.

Key Insight: Warren Buffett, the world's greatest investor, instructed his estate to put 90% of his wife's inheritance in an S&P 500 index fund - not his own Berkshire Hathaway stock. His words: "A low-cost index fund is the most sensible equity investment for the great majority of investors."

How Index Funds Work

  1. Index Selection: The fund chooses a benchmark (e.g., Nifty 50 with India's top 50 companies)
  2. Portfolio Mirroring: Invests in all 50 stocks in the exact same weightage as the index
  3. Automatic Rebalancing: When index composition changes, the fund adjusts automatically
  4. Minimal Trading: Buy-and-hold strategy means lower transaction costs

What Are ETFs (Exchange Traded Funds)?

An ETF is an investment fund that trades on stock exchanges like regular shares. Like index funds, most ETFs passively track an index, but they offer real-time trading flexibility and even lower expense ratios.

How ETFs Work

  1. Creation: Authorized Participants (APs) deposit underlying stocks with the fund house
  2. Units Created: AMC issues ETF units in exchange for these stocks
  3. Exchange Listing: ETF units trade on NSE/BSE like any stock
  4. Market Making: APs provide liquidity by buying/selling to keep prices near NAV
  5. Redemption: Large investors can redeem ETF units for underlying stocks

ETF Characteristics at a Glance

Feature ETF
Management Style Passive (rule-based)
Expense Ratio 0.02% - 0.10% (Lowest!)
Demat Required Yes (Mandatory)
SIP Available No (Manual purchases only)

Minimum Investment 1 unit (~Rs 250-300 for Nifty ETFs)
Brokerage Applicable (adds to cost)
Note on ETF Pricing: ETFs can trade at a slight premium or discount to NAV based on demand/supply. This is usually minimal for liquid ETFs like Nifty 50, but check the price vs NAV before buying large amounts.

Why Do "Expert" Fund Managers Underperform?

  1. Higher Expenses: 1.5-2% expense ratio means they need to beat the index by 1.5-2% BEFORE matching it
  2. Transaction Costs: Active trading = more brokerage, STT, impact costs
  3. Market Efficiency: Large-cap stocks are heavily researched - finding "hidden gems" is nearly impossible
  4. Cash Drag: Active funds hold 5-10% cash for redemptions, which drags returns
  5. Human Bias: Even experts make emotional decisions during market volatility

Understanding Tracking Error

Tracking error measures how closely an index fund/ETF follows its benchmark. Lower is better - it means the fund is doing its job of replicating the index.

What is a Good Tracking Error?

Tracking Error Rating Interpretation
Below 0.10% Excellent Near-perfect index replication
0.10% - 0.30% Good Acceptable for most investors
0.30% - 0.50% Average Monitor for improvement
0.50% - 1.00% Below Average Consider switching
Above 1.00% Poor Fund failing to track index

Why Does Tracking Error Occur?

  • Expense Ratio: Fund fees reduce returns vs the index
  • Cash Holdings: Funds hold 2-5% cash for redemptions
  • Transaction Costs: Brokerage and STT when rebalancing
  • Corporate Actions: Timing of dividend and bonus handling
  • Index Reconstitution: Delay in adjusting portfolio when index changes
SEBI Regulation: SEBI mandates that tracking error for equity ETFs and index funds cannot exceed 2%. Most well-managed funds maintain tracking error below 0.5%.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake #1: Choosing Regular Plan Over Direct Plan

Why it's a problem: Regular plans have 0.5-1% higher expense ratio for the same fund

Better approach: Always choose Direct Plan. Invest through AMC website or apps like Groww, Kuvera, Zerodha Coin

Mistake #2: Buying ETF Without Checking Liquidity

Why it's a problem: Low-volume ETFs may have wide bid-ask spreads, costing you money

Better approach: Stick to high-AUM ETFs like SBI Nifty 50 ETF, ICICI Nifty 50 ETF, or NIFTYBEES

Mistake #3: Ignoring Tracking Error

Why it's a problem: High tracking error means your fund isn't delivering index returns

Better approach: Check tracking error in the fund factsheet. Look for below 0.5%

Mistake #4: Timing the Market With Index Funds

Why it's a problem: Index investing is about consistent SIP, not timing entries

Better approach: Start SIP today. Time in market beats timing the market.

Mistake #5: Over-Diversifying Across Too Many Index Funds

Why it's a problem: 5 different Nifty 50 funds don't give more diversification - they're identical

Better approach: One fund per index. Diversify across DIFFERENT indices (Nifty 50, Next 50, S&P 500)

Frequently Asked Questions

What is an index fund?

An index fund is a type of mutual fund that passively tracks a market index like Nifty 50 by investing in the same stocks in the same proportion. It aims to deliver returns matching the index performance, minus a small expense ratio (typically 0.1-0.3% for direct plans). Index funds don't require a demat account and allow SIP investments starting Rs 500.

What is the difference between index fund and ETF?

Index funds are mutual funds traded at daily NAV, don't require demat accounts, and allow SIP investments. ETFs trade on stock exchanges like shares in real-time, require demat accounts, have lower expense ratios (0.02-0.05%), but don't support SIP. Both track the same indices but differ in trading mechanism, costs, and convenience.

Which is better - index fund or ETF for beginners?

Index funds are generally better for beginners because they don't require a demat account, allow SIP investments starting Rs 500, and offer simpler purchase/redemption at NAV. ETFs suit investors who already have demat accounts and want the lowest possible expense ratios (0.02-0.05%).

What is tracking error in index funds?

Tracking error measures how closely an index fund replicates its benchmark index returns. Lower tracking error (below 0.5%) indicates better index replication. SEBI mandates maximum 2% tracking error for equity index funds and ETFs in India. Causes include expense ratio, cash holdings, transaction costs, and rebalancing delays.

How much money do I need to start investing in index funds?

You can start investing in index funds with as little as Rs 100-500 through SIP (Systematic Investment Plan). Most AMCs offer minimum SIP of Rs 100-500 for index funds. For ETFs, you need to buy at least 1 unit (approximately Rs 250-300 for Nifty 50 ETFs).

What is the best Nifty 50 index fund to invest in India 2026?

Top Nifty 50 index funds for 2026 include: UTI Nifty 50 Index Fund (Rs 26,947 Cr AUM, 0.19% expense ratio), HDFC Nifty 50 Index Fund (Rs 22,718 Cr AUM, 0.20% expense ratio), and Nippon India Nifty 50 Index Fund (0.07% expense ratio - lowest). Select based on lowest expense ratio and tracking error.

Can I lose money in index funds?

Yes, index funds are subject to market risk. If the Nifty 50 falls 20%, your index fund will also fall approximately 20%. However, historical data shows Nifty 50 has delivered positive returns over all 7+ year periods with 10-Year CAGR of 12.64%, making long-term investing relatively safer.
